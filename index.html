Troubleshooting

Most Common Appliance Problems

These are the issues our technicians encounter most often—and the ones most homeowners can diagnose themselves.

Refrigerator Issues

  • ❄️
    Not Cooling Properly

    Often caused by dirty condenser coils, a faulty evaporator fan, or a failing compressor. Start by vacuuming coils at the back or bottom of the unit. If the freezer works but the fridge section is warm, the evaporator fan is the likely culprit. Compressor failure is more serious and typically requires a professional.

  • 💧
    Water Leaking Inside

    A clogged defrost drain is the most common cause. The drain can freeze or accumulate debris, causing water to pool at the bottom of the fridge compartment. Use a turkey baster with warm water to flush the drain. If the leak comes from the ice maker or water dispenser, check for cracked supply lines or loose fittings.

  • 🔊
    Loud or Unusual Noises

    Buzzing, clicking, or rattling sounds point to different components. A steady hum is normal compressor operation; loud buzzing may indicate a failing compressor relay. Rattling when the fridge cycles often means the condenser fan blade is hitting something. Clicking every few minutes typically signals a defrost timer or start relay issue.

  • 🌡️
    Temperature Control Problems

    If the refrigerator or freezer won't maintain temperature, suspect the thermostat, thermistor, or control board. A simple test: place a thermometer inside for a few hours. Fridge should read 35–38°F; freezer at 0°F. Significant deviation after adjusting the control dial points to a faulty thermostat.

Washer & Dryer Issues

  • 🚿
    Washer Not Draining

    Check the drain hose for kinks, and inspect the pump filter (usually behind a small access panel at the front bottom). A blocked lint filter is the single most common cause of slow or no draining. Clearing it takes about five minutes and no tools. If water remains after clearing the filter, the pump motor may need replacement.

  • ⚡
    Washer Not Spinning

    An unbalanced load often prevents the spin cycle from engaging—redistribute clothes and try again. Persistent no-spin issues usually point to a broken lid switch (top-loaders), door latch (front-loaders), worn carbon brushes on the motor, or a faulty capacitor. Most of these parts cost under $30 and are DIY-replaceable.

  • 🌬️
    Dryer Not Heating

    Always start with the lint trap and exhaust vent—a blocked vent is the number one cause of poor drying and a fire hazard. For electric dryers, a blown thermal fuse (about $10) cuts heating as a safety measure; check with a multimeter. Gas dryers that don't heat often have a faulty igniter or gas valve solenoid.

  • 💨
    Dryer Takes Too Long

    Slow drying is almost always a venting issue. Use a vent brush kit to clean the full length of the exhaust duct—even if the dryer is heating, restricted airflow dramatically extends cycle times. Also check the moisture sensor bars inside the drum; coating them with fine steel wool restores accuracy. A failing heating element can also reduce heat output.

DIY Quick Start

Essential Appliance Repair Skills Every Homeowner Should Know

You don't need to be a technician to handle many common appliance issues. With the right tools and knowledge, roughly 40% of all service calls can be avoided. Here are the foundational skills that pay off the most.

1. Using a Multimeter for Appliance Diagnosis

A digital multimeter is the single most valuable tool in any repair kit. At its most basic, it tells you whether electricity is flowing through a component. Use it to test heating elements, thermal fuses, door switches, and thermostats. Set the meter to resistance (ohms); a reading near zero means the component is functioning, while an infinite reading (or "OL") means it has failed and needs replacement. A decent multimeter costs $20–$40 and pays for itself the first time you use it.

2. Identifying and Ordering the Right Parts

Every appliance has a model number stamped on a label inside the door, on the back panel, or behind a removable drawer. Write it down and use it to search manufacturer parts sites or retailers like Repair Clinic, AppliancePartsPros, or Amazon. Always order by model number, not just part name — a "door switch" for a Whirlpool WTW4855HW is not interchangeable with one for a WTW4816FW, even though they look identical.

3. Safely Disconnecting Power and Gas

Before opening any appliance for repair, always disconnect it from its power source. For electric appliances, unplug from the wall or trip the dedicated breaker. For gas appliances, turn the shutoff valve on the supply line (usually behind the appliance) fully clockwise. Wait 5 minutes after disconnecting a gas appliance before working near any electrical components to allow residual gas to dissipate. Never skip this step — capacitors in microwaves and dryers can hold lethal charges for up to 30 minutes after unplugging.

4. Cleaning Condenser Coils and Drain Lines

Refrigerator condenser coils collect dust and pet hair over time, forcing the compressor to work harder and shortening its life. Clean them every 6–12 months using a coil brush and vacuum. The coils are located either at the back of the unit or behind a grille at the bottom front. For drain lines and dishwasher filters, a monthly rinse prevents blockages that lead to flooding and foul odors — tasks that take under 10 minutes but save hundreds in service calls.

5. Replacing Door Seals and Gaskets

A worn door seal on a refrigerator, oven, or dishwasher dramatically reduces efficiency and can cause secondary damage. Test fridge seals with the dollar-bill trick: close a bill in the door; if it slides out easily, the seal needs replacement. Replacement gaskets for most models cost $15–$50 and snap or screw into place without special tools. For ovens, a damaged door seal means heat escapes, leading to uneven cooking and higher energy bills.

🔧 Pro Tip: Take Photos Before Disassembly Use your smartphone to photograph wire harnesses, screw locations, and component orientations before removing anything. These photos are invaluable when reassembling and prevent costly mistakes.

6. Clearing Dryer Exhaust Vents

A clogged dryer exhaust vent is the leading cause of residential dryer fires — approximately 15,000 per year in the U.S. according to FEMA. It's also the most common reason dryers take excessively long to dry clothes. Disconnect the exhaust hose from the back of the dryer and use a long flexible vent brush to push debris through the duct. On the exterior, remove the vent cover and check for bird nests or crushed flex duct. Do this annually, or every six months if you do more than 5 loads per week.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does appliance repair cost on average?

Repair costs vary widely depending on the appliance and the fault. Refrigerator repairs average $200–$400, washing machine repairs run $150–$350, dryer repairs cost $100–$250, and oven or range repairs typically fall in the $150–$300 range. Labor rates vary by region and typically run $75–$150 per hour. Always request a written estimate before work begins — most reputable services offer free or low-cost diagnostic visits with repair.

Is it worth repairing a 10-year-old appliance?

The 50% Rule is a good starting point: if the cost to repair an appliance exceeds 50% of what it would cost to replace it new, replacement often makes more financial sense. However, age matters too — a 10-year-old refrigerator is past the midpoint of its expected lifespan, so a major repair like a compressor replacement may not make economic sense even if the repair cost is under 50%. For a 10-year-old appliance, we'd recommend repair only for minor issues under $200.

What appliance repairs should I never DIY?

Some repairs are genuinely dangerous without proper training and tools. Never attempt: refrigerant (Freon) work — this requires an EPA 608 certification; gas line connections or valve replacements; microwave magnetron or capacitor replacement (these hold lethal charges); any repair where you cannot completely isolate the appliance from its power source. For all other repairs, consult our DIY guides and proceed only if you're comfortable with the steps involved.

How long do appliance repairs take?

Simple repairs like replacing a thermal fuse, lid switch, or door gasket can be completed in 30–60 minutes. More complex repairs involving control boards, pumps, or compressors may take 2–3 hours, or require a return visit if parts need to be ordered. When booking a service call, we'll provide an estimated timeframe after the initial diagnostic. Most repairs are completed in a single visit, assuming parts are available.

Our technicians are trained to service all major brands including Whirlpool, Maytag, KitchenAid, GE, GE Profile, Frigidaire, Samsung, LG, Bosch, Electrolux, Miele, Sub-Zero, Thermador, and Wolf. For specialty or commercial brands, please call to confirm availability in your area. We carry OEM parts for over 200 appliance models and can order parts for most others within 1–2 business days.
